1896

Plessy v. Ferguson

The landmark Supreme Court case that established the "separate but equal" doctrine and institutionalized racial segregation in America

The Case That Divided America

Plessy v. Ferguson was a landmark 1896 U.S. Supreme Court decision that upheld the constitutionality of racial segregation under the "separate but equal" doctrine. The case stemmed from an 1892 incident in which African American train passenger Homer Plessy refused to sit in a Jim Crow car, breaking Louisiana's Separate Car Act.

Why This Case Mattered

The Plessy decision provided legal justification for segregation for the next 58 years, affecting schools, transportation, restaurants, and all public facilities. It wasn't until Brown v. Board of Education in 1954 that "separate but equal" was overturned. The case represents both the institutionalization of racism in America and the beginning of organized resistance to segregation.

Historical Timeline

1890

Louisiana's Separate Car Act

Louisiana passes a law requiring "equal but separate accommodations for the white and colored races" on railroads. The Comité des Citoyens (Committee of Citizens), a group of Creole professionals in New Orleans, forms to challenge the law.

1892

Homer Plessy's Arrest

On June 7, Homer Plessy, who was seven-eighths white and one-eighth Black, purchases a first-class ticket and sits in the whites-only car. When he refuses to move, he's arrested. The Comité des Citoyens had carefully planned this act of civil disobedience to test the law.

1896

Supreme Court Decision

On May 18, the U.S. Supreme Court rules 7-1 that state laws requiring racial segregation in public facilities are constitutional, establishing the "separate but equal" doctrine. Justice John Marshall Harlan writes the sole dissenting opinion.

1954

Overturned by Brown

In Brown v. Board of Education, the Supreme Court unanimously overturns Plessy, declaring that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal." This marks the beginning of the end for legal segregation in America.

Deep Dive: The Plessy Case and Its Legacy

The Plessy v. Ferguson case was not just about a train ride—it was a carefully orchestrated challenge to segregation laws with far-reaching consequences. Here's an in-depth look at various aspects of this pivotal moment in American history:

The Test Case Strategy

The Comité des Citoyens deliberately chose Homer Plessy because his light skin color demonstrated the absurdity of racial classifications. They hired a private detective to ensure Plessy would be arrested for sitting in the whites-only car.

Legal Arguments

Plessy's lawyers argued that the Separate Car Act violated the 13th Amendment (abolishing slavery) and 14th Amendment (equal protection). The Supreme Court rejected these arguments, claiming segregation didn't imply inferiority.

Justice Harlan's Dissent

The lone dissenter, Justice Harlan, famously wrote: "Our Constitution is color-blind." His dissent would later become the law of the land in Brown v. Board of Education.

Social Context

The decision came during the nadir of American race relations, with lynching peaking and Jim Crow laws spreading across the South. It emboldened segregationists and disenfranchised African Americans.

Long-Term Effects

For 58 years, Plessy justified segregation in schools, transportation, restaurants, and public spaces. The "equal" part of "separate but equal" was routinely ignored, with Black facilities being vastly inferior.

Modern Relevance

While overturned, Plessy's legacy persists in debates about school segregation, housing discrimination, and systemic racism. The case remains a cautionary tale about the Supreme Court's role in civil rights.

The Road to Brown

The NAACP's legal strategy to overturn Plessy began in the 1930s with cases challenging segregation in graduate schools. Thurgood Marshall and other civil rights lawyers carefully built precedents before directly attacking "separate but equal" in Brown. The 1954 decision didn't immediately end segregation but began the process that would culminate in the Civil Rights Act of 1964.

Key Figures

The plaintiff in the case, a shoemaker of mixed racial heritage who deliberately challenged Louisiana's segregation laws.

Albion Tourgée

Justice who wrote the majority opinion upholding the "separate but equal" doctrine.

The Morley-Minto Reforms

The Morley-Minto Reforms, also known as the Indian Councils Act of 1909, were introduced by Secretary of State John Morley and Viceroy Lord Minto. The reforms expanded the legislative councils and, for the first time, provided separate electorates for Muslims, a move that had a lasting impact on Indian politics.

Key Features

The reforms increased the number of elected Indian members in the councils, allowed for separate electorates for Muslims, and gave Indians a greater voice in governance, though real power remained with the British.

Deep Dive: Morley-Minto Reforms

The Morley-Minto Reforms were a response to growing demands for Indian representation. While they increased Indian participation, they also sowed the seeds of communal politics by introducing separate electorates.

Separate Electorates

Muslims could now elect their own representatives, a move that shaped future political developments.

Expansion of Councils

The number of members in legislative councils was increased, and more Indians were included.

Limitations

Despite reforms, real power remained with the British, and Indian demands for self-rule continued.

Political Impact

The reforms marked the beginning of communal representation in Indian politics.

Key Figures

John Morley, Lord Minto, Aga Khan III, Nawab Salimullah, Mohammad Ali Jinnah.

Legacy

The reforms are seen as a milestone in India's constitutional development, but also as a step towards communal division.

Legacy of the Morley-Minto Reforms

The reforms set a precedent for future constitutional changes, but also contributed to the communalization of Indian politics.

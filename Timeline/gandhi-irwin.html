Gandhi Irwin Pact

The Gandhi Irwin Pact of 1931 was an agreement between Mahatma Gandhi and the Viceroy Lord Irwin, marking a truce in the Civil Disobedience Movement and paving the way for the Second Round Table Conference.

The Gandhi Irwin Pact

Signed on 5 March 1931, the pact ended the first phase of the Civil Disobedience Movement. Gandhi agreed to suspend the movement, and the government agreed to release political prisoners and allow peaceful picketing.

Why was it important?

The pact was a significant step in negotiations between the Congress and the British government, though it did not meet all Congress demands.

Deep Dive: Gandhi Irwin Pact

The pact was a landmark in the freedom struggle, showing the effectiveness of negotiation and nonviolent protest, but also the limitations of such agreements under colonial rule.

Terms of the Pact

Release of political prisoners, right to peaceful picketing, and return of confiscated property.

Limitations

Many Congress demands, such as inquiry into police excesses and commutation of Bhagat Singh's sentence, were not met.

Impact

The pact paved the way for further negotiations and international attention to India's cause.

Key Figures

Mahatma Gandhi, Lord Irwin, Congress leaders.

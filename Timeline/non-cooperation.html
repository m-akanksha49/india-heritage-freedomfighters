Non-Cooperation Movement

The Non-Cooperation Movement, led by Mahatma Gandhi, was a mass protest against British rule, calling for Indians to withdraw from colonial institutions.

The Non-Cooperation Movement

Launched in 1920, the Non-Cooperation Movement was a nationwide campaign of nonviolent resistance against British rule. Indians were urged to boycott British goods, schools, courts, and titles, and to promote swadeshi and self-reliance.

Why Non-Cooperation?

The movement was a response to the Jallianwala Bagh Massacre, the Rowlatt Act, and the failure of the Montagu Chelmsford Reforms to meet nationalist aspirations.

Historical Timeline

1920

Movement Launched

Gandhi and the Congress launch the Non-Cooperation Movement, calling for mass boycott of British institutions.

1921

Mass Participation

Millions join the movement, boycotting schools, courts, and foreign goods, and promoting khadi and swadeshi.

1922

Chauri Chaura Incident

The movement is called off after violence erupts at Chauri Chaura, where protesters set fire to a police station.

Deep Dive: Non-Cooperation Movement

The movement marked the first mass participation of Indians in the freedom struggle, uniting people across regions and communities in nonviolent protest.

Boycott of Institutions

Indians left British schools, courts, and government jobs, and returned British honors and titles.

Promotion of Swadeshi

The movement encouraged the use of Indian-made goods and the spinning of khadi.

Chauri Chaura Incident

Violence at Chauri Chaura led Gandhi to call off the movement, emphasizing nonviolence.

Key Leaders

Mahatma Gandhi, Motilal Nehru, C.R. Das, Sardar Patel, Jawaharlal Nehru.

Legacy

The movement set the stage for future mass movements and established Gandhi as the leader of the national struggle.

Legacy of Non-Cooperation

The movement demonstrated the power of mass nonviolent protest and inspired future campaigns for independence.

June 22, 1897

The Chaphekar Brothers Assassination

The first political assassination of British colonial era - Damodar, Balkrishna and Vasudeo Chaphekar's killing of W.C. Rand

The Historic Assassination

On June 22, 1897, during Queen Victoria's Diamond Jubilee celebrations, brothers Damodar Hari (20), Balkrishna Hari (19), and Vasudeo Hari (16) Chaphekar assassinated British plague commissioner W.C. Rand and his military escort Lt. Ayerst in Pune. This was the first political assassination of the British colonial era in India, marking a radical turn in Indian resistance to colonial rule.

Background: The Plague Measures

The assassination was a direct response to the brutal measures taken by Rand during the 1896-97 bubonic plague epidemic in Pune. His Plague Committee forcibly entered homes, desecrated temples, segregated and hospitalized people against their will, and conducted humiliating physical examinations. These actions deeply offended Indian sensibilities and sparked outrage.

Deep Dive: The Assassination and Its Impact

The Chaphekar Brothers' assassination of W.C. Rand was a watershed moment in India's freedom struggle. Here's an in-depth examination of the event and its consequences:

Motivation Behind the Act

The brothers were influenced by nationalist writings, particularly those of Bal Gangadhar Tilak in Kesari. Rand's plague measures included forced home entries, stripping and examination of women by male doctors, destruction of property, and desecration of religious spaces - all of which were deeply humiliating to Pune's populace.

Planning the Assassination

The brothers planned meticulously for months. They learned to shoot at a local range, conducted surveillance on Rand's movements, and chose the Jubilee celebrations when security would be focused elsewhere. They waited near Ganeshkhind road where Rand's carriage would pass.

The Fateful Night

On June 22, 1897, Damodar fired at Rand's carriage but hit Lt. Ayerst instead. Balkrishna then shot Rand. In the confusion, they escaped. Ayerst died immediately while Rand succumbed to his wounds on June 26. The brothers left a note in Rand's pocket reading: "We have killed Rand. The other two brothers will kill the rest."

Investigation and Arrest

Police investigation initially went nowhere until they traced a marked currency note given to the brothers' associate. Damodar was arrested first in October 1897 after a dramatic chase. His confession led to Balkrishna's arrest. Vasudeo was arrested later after another chase where he attempted suicide.

Trial and Execution

The trial was conducted swiftly. Damodar and Balkrishna were sentenced to death and hanged on April 18, 1898. Vasudeo, being underage, received life imprisonment but died in prison. Their associate Mahadev Vinayak Ranade committed suicide before arrest.

Historical Significance

The assassination marked the beginning of revolutionary terrorism in India's freedom struggle. While condemned by moderates, it inspired later revolutionaries. Tilak's subsequent imprisonment for "seditious writings" seen as inspiring the act made him a nationalist martyr. The event showed British authorities the depth of Indian resentment against colonial policies.

Legacy and Controversy

The Chaphekar brothers remain controversial figures - seen either as violent terrorists or patriotic martyrs. Their act demonstrated that colonial oppression would meet violent resistance, forcing the British to moderate some policies. However, it also led to harsher repression including the 1898 amendment to the Indian Penal Code introducing tougher sedition laws. The brothers inspired later revolutionaries like Khudiram Bose and the Anushilan Samiti. Modern historians debate whether their act was justified resistance to tyranny or counterproductive violence. The British response to the plague and the assassination became a case study in how public health measures perceived as oppressive can backfire. The Chaphekars' sacrifice is commemorated in Pune with memorials, though their complex legacy continues to be reinterpreted.

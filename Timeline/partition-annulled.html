Partition of Bengal Annulled

After years of protests, boycotts, and demonstrations, the British government annulled the Partition of Bengal in 1911. The move was seen as a triumph for Indian unity and the Swadeshi movement, though it also led to the shifting of the capital from Calcutta to Delhi.

Why was it annulled?

The persistent and widespread opposition to the partition, along with the rise of national consciousness, forced the British to reverse their decision.

Deep Dive: Annulment of Partition

The annulment of the partition was a turning point in India's freedom struggle. It demonstrated the power of mass protest and unity, and set the stage for future movements.

Swadeshi Movement

The movement for indigenous goods and boycott of British products played a key role in the annulment.

National Unity

The partition, intended to divide, actually united people from different communities in a common cause.

Aftermath

The annulment was a victory, but also led to the shifting of the capital to Delhi and the rise of new political challenges.

Key Leaders

Chittaranjan Das, Rabindranath Tagore, Bipin Chandra Pal, Lord Curzon.

Legacy

The annulment is remembered as a triumph of people's power and a milestone in the freedom movement.

Legacy of the Annulment

The annulment of the partition inspired future generations to believe in the power of unity and protest.

The Formation of Anushilan Samiti

The Anushilan Samiti (Self-Culture Association) was formed in 1902 in Calcutta as a cultural organization that gradually transformed into one of India's most prominent revolutionary groups. Founded by Satish Chandra Basu with guidance from Sarala Devi Chaudhurani, it became the nucleus of revolutionary activities in Bengal.

Origins and Ideology

The Samiti was initially established to promote physical, mental, and spiritual development among Indian youth, inspired by Bankim Chandra Chatterjee's "Anushilan Tattva" (theory of discipline). Under the leadership of Barindra Kumar Ghosh (younger brother of Sri Aurobindo) and Bhupendranath Datta, it evolved into a secret revolutionary organization advocating armed resistance against British rule.

Barindra Kumar Ghosh and Bhupendranath Datta establish the more militant Jugantar group within Anushilan.

Anushilan members involved in plot to assassinate Viceroy Hardinge, leading to increased British repression.

Collaboration with German forces through the Hindu-German Conspiracy to procure arms for rebellion.

1930s

Decline and Transformation

Many members join mainstream politics or communist movements, while some continue revolutionary activities.

Deep Dive: The Revolutionary Network

Anushilan Samiti became the most extensive revolutionary network in colonial India, with branches across Bengal and connections with revolutionaries nationwide. Here's an in-depth examination of its structure and activities:

Organizational Structure

The Samiti operated through small, autonomous cells (5-10 members each) to maintain secrecy. It had a hierarchical structure with Barindra Ghosh and Bhupendranath Datta at the top. Members underwent rigorous physical training (lathi, sword, dagger) and ideological indoctrination. The organization published revolutionary literature including the newspaper Jugantar.

Key Activities

Anushilan specialized in "swadeshi dacoities" to fund operations, targeting British institutions and wealthy Indians. Members conducted surveillance on British officials and planned assassinations. They established bomb factories and trained in explosives. The Samiti maintained secret safe houses and communication networks across Bengal.

Major Operations

1908: Attempted assassination of Magistrate Kingsford in Muzaffarpur (leading to Alipore Bomb Case). 1912: Delhi-Lahore Conspiracy to assassinate Viceroy Hardinge. 1915: World War I collaboration with Germany (Hindu-German Conspiracy). Numerous attacks on British officials and police stations throughout Bengal.

Ideological Foundations

Influenced by Bankim Chandra's "Anushilan Tattva" and Bhagavad Gita's concept of righteous war. Combined nationalist fervor with spiritual discipline. Believed in armed resistance as necessary complement to non-violent movement. Drew inspiration from European revolutionary movements and Japanese militarism.

Notable Members

Barindra Kumar Ghosh (key organizer), Bhupendranath Datta (ideologue), Pulin Behari Das (Dhaka branch leader), Jatindranath Mukherjee (Bagha Jatin), Rash Behari Bose, Sachindranath Sanyal. Many members later joined Indian National Army or communist movement. Several became prominent in post-independence politics.

British Response

1908: Alipore Bomb Case trials resulted in executions and deportations. 1915: Defense of India Act used to suppress revolutionary activities. Extensive intelligence network established to infiltrate groups. Many leaders imprisoned in Cellular Jail, Andamans. Police Commissioner Charles Tegart became specialist in combating revolutionaries.

Legacy and Impact

Anushilan Samiti demonstrated the potential of organized revolutionary resistance against colonial rule. While ultimately unsuccessful in triggering mass uprising, it kept revolutionary tradition alive during moderate phase of Congress. Inspired later revolutionary groups across India including Hindustan Socialist Republican Association. Many techniques pioneered by Anushilan were adopted by later revolutionaries. The Samiti's emphasis on youth mobilization and secret organization became models for anti-colonial movements. Though controversial for its violent methods, Anushilan played crucial role in maintaining pressure on British administration. Its members' sacrifices became part of nationalist mythology, inspiring generations of freedom fighters. The organization's complex legacy continues to be debated by historians - as terrorists or patriots, as misguided youth or visionary revolutionaries.

Key Facts

  • Founded in 1902 in Calcutta by Satish Chandra Basu
  • Initially focused on physical and spiritual development
  • Evolved into secret revolutionary organization
  • Militant wing called Jugantar formed in 1906
  • Network spread across Bengal with Dhaka branch
  • Involved in numerous revolutionary activities
  • Precursor to later revolutionary movements

Methods and Techniques

  • Secret cell-based organization
  • Physical and weapons training
  • Swadeshi dacoities to fund operations
  • Bomb-making and assassination attempts
  • Underground literature and propaganda
  • International networking for arms
  • Use of coded communication

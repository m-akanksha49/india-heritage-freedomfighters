The Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

On April 13, 1919, British troops under General Dyer opened fire on a peaceful gathering at Jallianwala Bagh in Amritsar, killing hundreds of unarmed men, women, and children. The massacre shocked the nation and the world, fueling the Indian independence movement.

Why did it happen?

The massacre was a response to protests against the Rowlatt Act, which allowed the British to arrest and detain people without trial. The brutal crackdown was intended to suppress dissent but instead galvanized the freedom struggle.

Deep Dive: Jallianwala Bagh Massacre

The massacre marked a turning point in India's freedom struggle, uniting people against colonial rule and leading to the launch of the Non-Cooperation Movement.

Rowlatt Act

The repressive Rowlatt Act was the immediate cause of the protests that led to the massacre.

General Dyer's Actions

General Dyer's decision to fire without warning was widely condemned in India and abroad.

Impact on National Movement

The massacre led to a loss of faith in British justice and increased support for independence.

Hunter Commission

The commission's report was seen as a whitewash, further angering Indians.

Gandhi's Response

Gandhi called for non-cooperation with the British, leading to a mass movement.

Legacy

The massacre is remembered as one of the darkest chapters in colonial history and a catalyst for India's independence.

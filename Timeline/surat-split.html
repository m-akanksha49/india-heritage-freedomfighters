The Surat Split

The Surat Split occurred during the annual session of the Indian National Congress in 1907 at Surat. The Congress was divided into two groups: Moderates, led by Gopal Krishna Gokhale, and Extremists, led by Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Lala Lajpat Rai, and Bipin Chandra Pal. The split weakened the national movement temporarily but also paved the way for new strategies and leadership.

Why did the split happen?

The split was caused by differences in ideology and approach. Moderates favored constitutional methods and gradual reforms, while Extremists advocated for direct action and self-rule.

Deep Dive: The Surat Split

The Surat Split was a significant event in the history of the Indian National Congress. It highlighted the ideological differences within the party and set the stage for future developments in the freedom movement.

Moderates vs. Extremists

Moderates believed in petitions, meetings, and discussions, while Extremists wanted immediate self-rule and were willing to use more assertive methods.

Impact on the Movement

The split weakened the Congress for a few years, but also led to the rise of new leaders and strategies.

Reunion at Lucknow

The reunion in 1916 brought together the strengths of both groups, leading to greater unity and effectiveness.

Key Leaders

Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Lala Lajpat Rai, Bipin Chandra Pal (Extremists); Gopal Krishna Gokhale (Moderates).

Legacy

The Surat Split is remembered as a turning point that shaped the future course of the Indian freedom struggle.

Legacy of the Surat Split

The Surat Split demonstrated the challenges of unity in a diverse movement, but also the resilience and adaptability of Indian leaders in the face of adversity.

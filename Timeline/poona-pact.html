The Poona Pact

Signed on 24 September 1932, the Poona Pact was a compromise between Dr. B.R. Ambedkar and Mahatma Gandhi regarding the representation of the depressed classes (Scheduled Castes) in British Indian legislatures.

Why was it important?

The pact averted the division of Hindu society and provided political representation to the depressed classes, shaping the future of affirmative action in India.

Deep Dive: Poona Pact

The Poona Pact was a turning point in the struggle for social justice, balancing the interests of the depressed classes and the unity of Hindu society.

Background

The Communal Award of 1932 proposed separate electorates for the depressed classes, which Gandhi opposed through a fast unto death.

Terms of the Pact

Reserved seats for the depressed classes in provincial legislatures, but within the general electorate.

Impact

The pact influenced the reservation policy in independent India.

Key Figures

Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, Mahatma Gandhi, Madan Mohan Malaviya.

Legacy

The pact is seen as a milestone in the movement for social justice and equality.

Legacy of the Poona Pact

The First National Political Organization

The Indian National Association (also known as Indian Association) was founded in 1876 by Surendranath Banerjee and Ananda Mohan Bose in Calcutta. It was the first avowed nationalist organization founded in British India, preceding the Indian National Congress by about a decade. The Association aimed to represent Indian interests and promote political advancement of Indians under British rule.

Objectives and Activities

The Indian National Association sought to create a strong public opinion on political questions, unify Indian people on a common political program, and promote friendly relations between Hindus and Muslims. It organized annual conferences that served as precursors to the Indian National Congress sessions. The Association campaigned against racial discrimination, demanded Indianization of government services, and advocated for constitutional reforms.

The Association holds its first annual conference in Calcutta, attended by delegates from across India.

The Association leads protests against the repressive Vernacular Press Act imposed by Lord Lytton.

Ilbert Bill Agitation

Plays key role in supporting the Ilbert Bill which proposed allowing Indian judges to try Europeans.

Indian National Congress

Many members of the Indian National Association join the newly formed Indian National Congress.

Merger

The Association merges with the Indian National Congress, though continues some independent activities.

Deep Dive: The Association's Role and Legacy

The Indian National Association played a pioneering role in India's political awakening. Here's an in-depth look at its contributions and historical significance:

Political Awakening

The Association marked the beginning of organized political activity in India. It created a platform for Indians to voice their grievances and demand political reforms from the British rulers.

Nationalist Ideals

It promoted the idea of India as a nation, transcending regional and religious differences. The annual conferences brought together leaders from different parts of the country.

Social Reforms

While primarily political, the Association also worked for social reforms including women's education and removal of caste barriers, though within moderate limits.

Precursor to Congress

The Association's annual conferences served as the model for the Indian National Congress sessions. Many of its members became prominent Congress leaders.

Constitutional Methods

It established the tradition of using petitions, resolutions and public meetings to press for reforms - methods later adopted by the Congress in its early phase.

Limitations

The Association remained largely confined to Bengal and had limited mass appeal. Its moderate approach was later challenged by more radical nationalists.

Historical Significance

The Indian National Association represents the first organized expression of Indian nationalism under British rule. While it was eventually overshadowed by the Indian National Congress, it laid important groundwork for India's freedom struggle. The Association demonstrated that Indians could organize politically to demand their rights, setting precedents for constitutional agitation that would characterize the early nationalist movement. Its leaders, particularly Surendranath Banerjee, became important figures in the development of nationalist thought. The Association's emphasis on Hindu-Muslim unity and its pan-Indian aspirations, though limited in practice, pointed toward the more comprehensive nationalism that would emerge in the 20th century.

Surendranath Banerjee

Principal founder of the Association, known as the "father of Indian nationalism".

Ananda Mohan Bose

Co-founder, first Indian Wrangler at Cambridge, and prominent social reformer.

Dwarkanath Ganguly

Active member who linked the Association with social reform movements.

Prominent leader and editor who advocated through the Hindu Patriot.

Major Achievements

  • First organized expression of Indian nationalism
  • Created pan-Indian political platform
  • Campaign against Vernacular Press Act (1878)
  • Advocacy for Indian civil service reforms
  • Opposition to lowering age limit for ICS exams
  • Support for Ilbert Bill (1883)
  • Model for Indian National Congress

Demands and Resolutions

  • Indianization of government services
  • Simultaneous civil service exams in India and England
  • Separation of executive and judicial functions
  • Repeal of Arms Act
  • Greater representation in legislative councils
  • Freedom of press and speech
  • Reduction of military expenditure

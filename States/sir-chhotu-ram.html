Chhotu Ram

A visionary reformer and voice of the farmers, Chhotu Ram fought for agrarian justice and upliftment of peasants during British rule.

Born

November 24, 1881

Garhi Sampla, Rohtak, Punjab Province (now Haryana)

Died

January 9, 1945 (aged 63)

Lahore, Punjab Province

Party

Unionist Party

Known For

Farmer rights, agrarian reforms, anti-zamindari laws

"A farmer does not beg, he feeds the world — let him live with dignity."

Biography

Chhotu Ram, born as Rai Bahadur Chhotu Ram, was one of the most influential social reformers during British India. Though not a part of violent revolution, his contribution to Indian society was massive—especially in the empowerment of peasants, Jats, and rural communities of Punjab and Haryana.

He worked relentlessly to draft and enforce pro-farmer laws and policies that protected small landowners from exploitation by zamindars and moneylenders. He served as Revenue Minister in the Punjab Province under British India.

Key Reforms

1935

Drafted the Punjab Relief of Indebtedness Act to free farmers from moneylender exploitation.

1936

Enacted the Punjab Debtors Protection Act and ensured fair land rights to tenants and farmers.

1930s

Promoted cooperative farming, rural banks, and equitable distribution of land and credit.

Legacy

Farmers’ Messiah

Lovingly called the “Messiah of Farmers,” he gave legal and political voice to rural India.

Statues & Colleges

Many colleges and statues bear his name, including Chhotu Ram University of Science and Technology.

Political Visionary

Co-founder of the Unionist Party — a secular platform representing Hindus, Muslims, and Sikhs in rural Punjab.

Social Reformer

Worked for caste equality and education of backward communities in Haryana and Punjab.

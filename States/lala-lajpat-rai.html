Lala Lajpat Rai

The Lion of Punjab - A fearless nationalist leader who gave his life for India's freedom.

Born

January 28, 1865

Dhudike, Ferozepur, Punjab

Died

November 17, 1928 (aged 63)

Lahore, Punjab

Organizations

Indian National Congress, Arya Samaj

Known For

Lal-Bal-Pal trio, Simon Commission protest

"I declare that the blows struck at me today will be the last nails in the coffin of British rule in India."

Biography

Lala Lajpat Rai was born on January 28, 1865, in Dhudike village, Ferozepur district, Punjab. He came from a family of dedicated teachers and was deeply influenced by the values of education and social reform from an early age.

He enrolled in Government College in Lahore in 1880 to study law, where he met future freedom fighters like Lala Hans Raj and Pandit Guru Dutt. During his time in Lahore, he was inspired by Swami Dayanand Saraswati's Hindu reformist movement and joined the Arya Samaj.

After completing his law degree, he started his legal practice in Hissar. His commitment to social reform and nationalist causes soon made him a prominent figure in Punjab's political landscape.

Political Career

1885

Joined the Indian National Congress and became an active member of the organization.

1905

Played a crucial role in the protests against the partition of Bengal, establishing himself as a prominent nationalist leader.

1907

Formed the famous "Lal-Bal-Pal" trio with Bal Gangadhar Tilak and Bipin Chandra Pal. Was deported to Burma without trial but returned after a few months due to lack of evidence.

1917-1920

Spent time in the United States, where he worked to garner international support for India's independence movement.

1928

Led the protest against the Simon Commission in Lahore. Sustained fatal injuries from police lathi charge and died on November 17, 1928.

The Lal-Bal-Pal Trio

Lala Lajpat Rai was one of the three prominent extremist leaders in the Indian National Congress, along with Bal Gangadhar Tilak from Maharashtra and Bipin Chandra Pal from Bengal. This trio was known as "Lal-Bal-Pal" and represented the militant wing of the Congress.

The trio advocated for:

  • Complete independence (Purna Swaraj) rather than dominion status
  • Boycott of British goods and institutions
  • Swadeshi movement to promote Indian products
  • National education system
  • Mass agitation and protests against British policies
"The sword of revolution is sharpened on the whetting-stone of ideas."

Social Reform Activities

Beyond politics, Lala Lajpat Rai was deeply committed to social reform:

  • Active member of the Arya Samaj, promoting Hindu religious reform
  • Fought against untouchability and caste discrimination
  • Advocated for women's education and rights
  • Promoted the study of Vedas for all sections of society
  • Established schools and educational institutions
  • Founded the Hisar district branch of the Indian National Congress

Literary and Journalistic Work

Lala Lajpat Rai was also a prolific writer and journalist who used his pen as a weapon against colonial rule:

  • Regular contributor to several newspapers including The Tribune
  • Wrote extensively on political and social issues
  • Authored biographies of great Indian leaders
  • His writings helped shape public opinion and political discourse
  • Used journalism to promote nationalist ideas and social reform

The Simon Commission Protest and Martyrdom

The Simon Commission was appointed by the British government in 1927 to review the constitutional reforms in India. However, it had no Indian members, which led to widespread protests across the country with the slogan "Simon Go Back!"

On October 30, 1928, when the Simon Commission arrived in Lahore, Lala Lajpat Rai led a massive protest demonstration. During the protest, British police led by Superintendent James Scott brutally lathi-charged the peaceful protesters. Lajpat Rai was severely beaten and sustained critical head injuries.

Despite his injuries, he addressed the crowd at Mochi Gate that same evening and made his famous declaration: "I declare that the blows struck at me today will be the last nails in the coffin of British rule in India."

Lajpat Rai never fully recovered from his injuries and died on November 17, 1928. His death sparked nationwide outrage and became a turning point in the Indian independence movement.

"Every blow on my body will prove to be the last nail in the coffin of British rule in India."

Legacy

Memorials

Numerous roads, institutions, and memorials across India are named after him. The Lala Lajpat Rai University in Hisar, Haryana, is a testament to his educational vision.

Inspiration

His martyrdom inspired the revolutionary activities of Bhagat Singh, who sought to avenge his death by assassinating British officer John Saunders.

Literary Legacy

His writings on nationalism, social reform, and political philosophy continue to be studied and appreciated by scholars and students.

Title

Remembered as the "Lion of Punjab" for his fearless leadership and ultimate sacrifice for the nation's freedom.

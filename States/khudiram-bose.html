Biography

Born on December 3, 1889 in Habibpur village (Midnapore district), Khudiram Bose was drawn to revolutionary activities from his teenage years. Orphaned at a young age, he joined Anushilan Samiti, a revolutionary organization in Bengal, when he was just 15.

In 1908, at age 18, he was assigned to assassinate Kingsford, the notorious British Chief Magistrate of Muzaffarpur known for harsh treatment of revolutionaries. On April 30, 1908, Bose and Prafulla Chaki threw bombs at a carriage they believed carried Kingsford, but it turned out to be a different European family, killing two British women accidentally.

After a dramatic chase, Bose was arrested while Chaki committed suicide. His trial became sensational across India. Despite being offered clemency if he revealed his associates, Bose refused. He was hanged on August 11, 1908, becoming one of India's youngest martyrs. His courage inspired generations of freedom fighters.

Cultural Legacy

Khudiram Bose lives on in India's collective memory through:

  • Folk Songs: Bengali ballads celebrating his martyrdom
  • Literature: Subject of poems, novels and plays
  • Films: Featured in movies like "Khudiram Bose" (1960)
  • Art: Paintings and sketches of his iconic trial scene
  • Oral History: Stories passed down generations in Bengal

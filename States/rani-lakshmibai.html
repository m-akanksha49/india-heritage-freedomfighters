Rani Lakshmi Bai

The fearless queen of Jhansi who became a symbol of resistance against British rule during the Indian Rebellion of 1857.

Born

November 19, 1828

Varanasi, British India

Died

June 18, 1858 (aged 29)

Gwalior, British India

Spouse

Maharaja Gangadhar Rao Newalkar

Known For

Leading the Indian Rebellion of 1857 in Jhansi

"I shall not surrender my Jhansi. If you want it, come and take it!"

Biography

Rani Lakshmi Bai, originally named Manikarnika Tambe, was born on November 19, 1828, in Varanasi to Moropant Tambe and Bhagirathi Sapre. She was affectionately called "Manu" in her childhood. Unlike most girls of her time, she was educated and trained in martial arts, horse riding, and sword fighting.

At the age of 14, she was married to Maharaja Gangadhar Rao Newalkar, the ruler of Jhansi. She gave birth to a son in 1851 who died after four months. Later, the couple adopted a son named Damodar Rao. After her husband's death in 1853, the British East India Company, under Governor-General Lord Dalhousie, applied the Doctrine of Lapse to annex Jhansi, refusing to recognize her adopted son as the heir.

This injustice led Rani Lakshmi Bai to take up arms against the British, making her one of the leading figures of the Indian Rebellion of 1857.

Role in the 1857 Rebellion

1853

After her husband's death, the British refused to recognize her adopted son as heir and annexed Jhansi under the Doctrine of Lapse.

1857

When the Indian Rebellion broke out in May 1857, she was initially hesitant but eventually took charge of Jhansi's defense after British forces massacred civilians.

1858

Led Jhansi's forces against the British siege in March-April 1858, personally supervising the defenses and encouraging her troops.

1858

After Jhansi fell, she escaped to Kalpi and joined other rebel leaders. She died fighting British troops near Gwalior on June 18, 1858.

Military Leadership

Rani Lakshmi Bai displayed exceptional military leadership during the 1857 rebellion:

  • Organized a strong defense of Jhansi against the British siege
  • Personally trained and led a volunteer army of men and women
  • Masterfully used guerrilla warfare tactics against better-equipped British forces
  • Formed alliances with other rebel leaders like Tantia Tope and Nana Sahib
  • Continued to fight even after the fall of Jhansi, participating in the defense of Kalpi and the capture of Gwalior

Her bravery and military acumen earned her the respect of both her followers and British opponents.

"The British described her as 'the most dangerous of all Indian leaders' for her courage, intelligence and persistence."

Legacy

Memorials

Numerous statues across India, including at Jhansi Fort and Jaipur. The Rani Mahal in Jhansi has been converted into a museum.

Cultural Impact

Subject of poems, novels, films and TV series. The famous poem "Khoob ladi mardani..." by Subhadra Kumari Chauhan celebrates her bravery.

Feminist Icon

Considered an early feminist icon in India for breaking gender stereotypes and leading armies in battle.

Honors

Indian Navy's first women-only sailing vessel INSV Tarini has a boat named "Mhadei" after her. Several institutions and awards bear her name.

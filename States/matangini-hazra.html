Matangini Hazra

The fearless revolutionary known as "Gandhi Buri" (Old Lady Gandhi) who sacrificed her life holding the Indian flag during the Quit India Movement.

Born

October 19, 1870

Hogla village, Tamluk, Bengal Presidency, British India

Died

September 29, 1942 (aged 71)

Tamluk, Midnapore, Bengal Presidency, British India

Movement

Quit India Movement, Civil Disobedience Movement, Non-Cooperation Movement

Known For

Martyrdom during Quit India Movement, Leading protests at age 71

"Vande Mataram! I shall not move an inch even if you shoot me!"

Biography

Matangini Hazra was born on October 19, 1870, in the small village of Hogla near Tamluk in Midnapore district (now in West Bengal). Married off at the young age of 12, she was widowed by 18 and had no children. Despite her lack of formal education, she became deeply involved in India's freedom struggle in her later years.

Inspired by Mahatma Gandhi's call for independence, the elderly Matangini became an active participant in the Indian independence movement. She earned the nickname "Gandhi Buri" (Old Lady Gandhi) for her dedication to Gandhian principles and her simple white khadi sari resembling Gandhi's attire.

Her greatest moment came during the Quit India Movement in 1942 when, at age 71, she led a procession of 6,000 protesters, mostly women, to take over the Tamluk police station. This act of defiance would become her final and most famous contribution to India's freedom struggle.

Revolutionary Activities

1905

First became politically active during the Swadeshi Movement against the partition of Bengal.

1920s

Joined the Indian National Congress and participated in the Non-Cooperation Movement, Salt Satyagraha, and Civil Disobedience Movement.

1932

Arrested for breaking the Salt Act and sentenced to six months imprisonment at Baharampur Jail.

1933

Attended the subdivisional Congress conference at Serampore and was injured in police lathi charge.

1942

Played a leading role in the Quit India Movement in Midnapore district.

September 29, 1942

Led a procession of 6,000 protesters to occupy the Tamluk police station. Shot three times by British police but continued marching with the Indian flag until she collapsed, dying with "Vande Mataram" on her lips.

Sacrifice and Martyrdom

On September 29, 1942, during the Quit India Movement, 71-year-old Matangini Hazra led a procession of about 6,000 protesters, mostly women, to take over the Tamluk police station. As they marched, British forces ordered them to disperse, and when they refused, the police opened fire.

Matangini was shot in the forehead, arm, and hand, but she continued marching forward, holding the Indian flag high and chanting "Vande Mataram." She collapsed only after being hit by the third bullet, dying with the flag still in her hands and patriotic slogans on her lips.

Her martyrdom became a rallying point for the independence movement in Bengal and inspired many others to join the struggle against British rule.

"The sacrifice of Matangini Hazra lit a fire in the hearts of Indians that no British bullet could extinguish."

Legacy

Memorials

Several statues across West Bengal, including one at the spot where she was martyred. The Tamluk police station she tried to occupy is now named Matangini Hazra Police Station.

Honors

Featured on an Indian postage stamp in 2002. The Matangini Hazra Award is West Bengal's highest state award for women.

Cultural Depictions

Featured in films like "Gandhi" (1982) and "The Legend of Bhagat Singh" (2002). Subject of numerous books and folk songs in Bengal.

Places Named

Matangini Hazra Road in Kolkata, Matangini Hazra College in Purba Medinipur, and many schools across West Bengal bear her name.

Mahatma Gandhi

The Father of the Nation who led India to independence through non-violent civil disobedience.

Born

October 2, 1869

Porbandar, Gujarat, British India

Died

January 30, 1948 (aged 78)

Movements

Leading India to independence, Philosophy of non-violence (Ahimsa)

"Be the change that you wish to see in the world."

Biography

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was born on October 2, 1869, in Porbandar, a coastal town in present-day Gujarat, India. He studied law in London and later practiced in South Africa, where he developed his political views and ethics, leading the Indian community there in civil disobedience against racial discrimination.

Returning to India in 1915, Gandhi organized peasants, farmers, and urban laborers to protest against excessive land-tax and discrimination. Assuming leadership of the Indian National Congress in 1921, he led nationwide campaigns for easing poverty, expanding women's rights, building religious and ethnic amity, ending untouchability, and achieving self-rule.

Gandhi's non-violent resistance led to India's independence in 1947, inspiring civil rights movements worldwide. He is revered as the Father of the Nation in India and honored globally for his philosophy of non-violence (ahimsa).

Key Movements and Events

1893-1914

Developed concept of Satyagraha (non-violent resistance) while fighting for civil rights of Indians in South Africa.

1917

Champaran Satyagraha - First civil disobedience movement in India supporting indigo farmers.

1919

Non-cooperation movement against British rule after the Jallianwala Bagh massacre.

1930

Salt March (Dandi March) - 240-mile march to produce salt from seawater in defiance of British salt laws.

1942

Quit India Movement - Mass protest demanding immediate British withdrawal from India.

1947

India gains independence on August 15 after decades of non-violent struggle led by Gandhi.

1948

Assassinated on January 30 by Nathuram Godse, a Hindu nationalist who opposed Gandhi's tolerance towards Muslims.

Philosophy and Principles

Gandhi's philosophy was based on several key principles:

  • Ahimsa (Non-violence): Complete non-violence in thought, word and deed
  • Satyagraha (Truth force): Civil disobedience through non-violent resistance
  • Swaraj (Self-rule): Both political independence and personal self-control
  • Swadeshi (Self-sufficiency): Economic independence through local production
  • Sarvodaya (Welfare of all): Upliftment of all sections of society
  • Trusteeship: Wealthy should consider themselves trustees of their wealth for society's benefit
"An eye for an eye only ends up making the whole world blind."

Legacy

Memorials

Raj Ghat in Delhi marks Gandhi's cremation spot. Numerous statues, roads, and institutions worldwide bear his name, including over 100 major roads in India renamed "MG Road".

Global Influence

Inspired civil rights leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela. The UN declared October 2 as International Day of Non-Violence.

Writings

Authored numerous works including "Hind Swaraj" and his autobiography "The Story of My Experiments with Truth". Collected works span 100 volumes.

Gandhi Jayanti

October 2, Gandhi's birthday, is a national holiday in India and celebrated worldwide as the International Day of Non-Violence.

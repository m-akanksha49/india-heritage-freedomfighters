Sheikh Mohammed Abdullah

The "Lion of Kashmir" who fought for the rights of Kashmiri people and served as the first elected Prime Minister of Jammu and Kashmir.

Born

December 5, 1905

Soura, Jammu and Kashmir, British India

Died

September 8, 1982 (aged 76)

Srinagar, Jammu and Kashmir, India

Political Party

National Conference (formerly Muslim Conference)

Known For

Kashmir's accession to India, Land reforms, Advocate of Kashmiri autonomy

"The people of Jammu and Kashmir have made their decision, and their decision is final."

Biography

Sheikh Mohammed Abdullah was born on December 5, 1905, in Soura, a village near Srinagar in the princely state of Jammu and Kashmir. Born into a middle-class family, he excelled in his studies and earned degrees from Islamia College, Lahore and Aligarh Muslim University. His political awakening began during his student days when he witnessed the discrimination faced by Kashmiri Muslims under the Dogra regime.

After returning to Kashmir in 1930, Abdullah became a school teacher but soon turned to political activism. In 1932, he founded the All Jammu and Kashmir Muslim Conference, which later evolved into the secular National Conference in 1939. His charismatic leadership and advocacy for land reforms earned him immense popularity among Kashmir's peasantry.

Abdullah played a pivotal role in Kashmir's accession to India in 1947 and became the first elected Prime Minister of Jammu and Kashmir. His relationship with the Indian government fluctuated between cooperation and conflict over the issue of Kashmir's autonomy. After periods of imprisonment, he returned as Chief Minister in 1975 under the Indira-Abdullah Accord.

Key Movements and Events

1931

First major political speech at Khanqah-e-Moula in Srinagar, marking the beginning of his political career.

1932

Founded the All Jammu and Kashmir Muslim Conference to fight against the autocratic Dogra rule.

1939

Transformed the Muslim Conference into the secular National Conference, adopting the "Naya Kashmir" manifesto.

1946

Launched the "Quit Kashmir" movement against the Dogra monarchy, leading to his arrest.

1947

Played crucial role in Kashmir's accession to India during the Partition; became Prime Minister of J&K.

1953

Dismissed and arrested due to differences over Kashmir's autonomy; remained in prison until 1964.

1965

Arrested again after returning from Algeria where he had met Chinese and Pakistani leaders.

1975

Returned as Chief Minister under the Indira-Abdullah Accord, remaining in office until his death.

Political Philosophy and Reforms

Sheikh Abdullah's political vision was shaped by his commitment to secularism, socialism, and Kashmiri identity:

  • Naya Kashmir (New Kashmir): A comprehensive program for socio-economic reforms including land redistribution
  • Land to the Tiller: Abolished feudal landlordism through radical land reforms in 1950
  • Secularism: Advocated a secular Jammu and Kashmir where all religions would be equal
  • Autonomy: Championed Article 370 and special status for J&K within Indian constitution
  • Democratic Socialism: Implemented progressive labor laws and workers' rights
  • Education: Established schools, colleges and the University of Kashmir
"Our fight is against poverty, against oppression, against ignorance, against disease."

Legacy

Political Dynasty

Founded a political dynasty that continues to dominate Kashmiri politics through his son Farooq Abdullah and grandson Omar Abdullah.

Institutions

Established key institutions including University of Kashmir, Sher-e-Kashmir Institute of Medical Sciences, and the Sher-e-Kashmir University of Agricultural Sciences.

Writings

Authored his autobiography "Aatish-e-Chinar" (Flames of the Chinar) which provides insights into Kashmir's political history.

Memorials

Buried at the Hazratbal shrine complex; memorials include the Sheikh Abdullah Bridge and numerous roads, hospitals and institutions named after him.

Controversies and Complex Legacy

Sheikh Abdullah's legacy remains complex and contested:

  • Praised for land reforms and empowerment of Kashmiri masses but criticized for dynastic politics
  • Initially advocated plebiscite but later supported accession to India
  • His changing stance on autonomy created tensions with both India and Pakistan
  • Periods of imprisonment made him both a martyr and a controversial figure
  • 1975 accord with Indira Gandhi was seen by some as compromise of Kashmiri aspirations
"I have spent 22 years in prison fighting for the rights of my people. That should be testimony enough of my commitment."

Usha Mehta

The courageous freedom fighter who ran the secret Congress Radio during the Quit India Movement, becoming the voice of India's independence struggle.

Born

March 25, 1920

Saras, Gujarat, British India

Died

August 11, 2000 (aged 80)

Mumbai, Maharashtra, India

Known For

Congress Radio, Quit India Movement

Awards

Padma Vibhushan (1998)

"We wanted to keep the flame of freedom burning through our broadcasts, to remind people that the fight was still on."

Biography

Usha Mehta was born on March 25, 1920, in Saras village near Surat in Gujarat. From a very young age, she was drawn to the Indian independence movement, participating in her first protest at just eight years old.

She completed her education in Bombay (now Mumbai), earning a BA in Philosophy and later an LLB. However, her true passion lay in the freedom struggle. During the Quit India Movement in 1942, at just 22 years old, she became famous for operating the underground Congress Radio, which became a crucial tool for communication among freedom fighters when the British had banned all Congress activities and censored the press.

After independence, she completed her PhD and became a professor of Political Science at the University of Bombay, where she taught for many years.

Freedom Struggle Contributions

1928

At just 8 years old, participated in her first protest against British rule, carrying water for the satyagrahis during the Simon Commission boycott.

1930

At age 10, participated in the protests against the Salt Laws during the Salt Satyagraha.

1942

Played a pivotal role in the Quit India Movement by establishing the secret Congress Radio along with other freedom fighters like Vithalbhai Jhaveri and Chandrakant Jhaveri.

1942

Arrested on November 12, 1942, for operating the illegal radio station. Spent four years in prison until 1946.

1998

Awarded the Padma Vibhushan, India's second-highest civilian award, for her contributions to the freedom struggle.

Congress Radio

Usha Mehta's most significant contribution to the freedom struggle was the establishment and operation of the secret Congress Radio during the Quit India Movement in 1942. When all Congress leaders were arrested and the organization banned, this underground radio station became the voice of the independence movement.

Key facts about Congress Radio:

  • First broadcast on August 27, 1942 from Bombay
  • Broadcast news about the freedom movement that was censored in mainstream media
  • Changed locations frequently to avoid detection
  • Broadcast messages from underground leaders
  • Operated for about three months before being discovered
  • Used the tagline "This is the Congress Radio calling on 42.34 meters from somewhere in India"
"The radio was our weapon against the British propaganda. Through it, we could tell our people the truth about our movement."

Legacy

Media Pioneer

Recognized as a pioneer of underground broadcasting in India. Her innovative use of radio inspired future generations of journalists and communicators.

Women in Freedom Struggle

Symbol of women's participation in the independence movement, showing how young women played crucial roles beyond traditional expectations.

Academic Contributions

After independence, made significant contributions to political science academia, bridging the gap between activism and scholarship.

Honors

Received numerous awards including the Padma Vibhushan. Several institutions and awards are named after her today.

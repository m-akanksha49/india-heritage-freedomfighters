Bhagat Singh

The revolutionary hero of India's independence movement who became a symbol of bravery and sacrifice.

Born

September 28, 1907

Bangay, Punjab, British India

Died

March 23, 1931 (aged 23)

Lahore, Punjab, British India

Organizations

Hindustan Socialist Republican Association

Known For

Indian independence movement, Revolutionary activities

"They may kill me, but they cannot kill my ideas. They can crush my body, but they will not be able to crush my spirit."

Biography

Bhagat Singh was born on September 28, 1907, in the village of Banga in the Lyallpur district of Punjab (now in Pakistan) to Kishan Singh and Vidyavati. From an early age, he was deeply influenced by the nationalist movement and the sacrifices made by his family members for India's freedom.

Bhagat Singh was a brilliant student and voracious reader who was deeply influenced by socialist and anarchist ideas. He joined the National College in Lahore where he came into contact with other revolutionaries and became involved in revolutionary activities against British rule.

He became one of the most prominent faces of the Indian independence movement through his revolutionary actions and writings that inspired millions of Indians to join the freedom struggle.

Revolutionary Activities

1928

Formed the Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA) with other revolutionaries like Chandrashekhar Azad and Sukhdev.

1928

Participated in the assassination of British police officer John Saunders to avenge the death of Lala Lajpat Rai during a protest against the Simon Commission.

1929

Along with Batukeshwar Dutt, threw bombs in the Central Legislative Assembly in Delhi to protest against repressive laws. They deliberately courted arrest to use the trial as a platform to propagate their ideas.

1931

Executed at the age of 23 along with Rajguru and Sukhdev on March 23 in Lahore Central Jail. Their execution sparked nationwide protests and turned them into martyrs of the Indian independence movement.

Ideology and Philosophy

Bhagat Singh was deeply influenced by socialist and anarchist thought. He believed in:

  • Complete independence from British rule
  • Establishment of a socialist society
  • Secularism and communal harmony
  • Empowerment of workers and peasants
  • Revolutionary methods to overthrow colonial rule

During his trial and imprisonment, he wrote extensively about his vision for India and the need for social revolution alongside political independence.

"The sword of revolution is sharpened on the whetting stone of ideas."

Legacy

Memorials

Numerous memorials across India including the National Martyrs Memorial at Hussainiwala, statues in Parliament and various cities, and museums dedicated to his life.

Cultural Impact

Subject of numerous films, plays, songs, and literature. Considered one of the most iconic freedom fighters in Indian popular culture.

Writings

His jail diaries and essays like "Why I Am an Atheist" continue to inspire generations of Indians and are studied widely.

Shaheed Diwas

March 23 is observed as Martyrs' Day (Shaheed Diwas) in India to honor Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev.

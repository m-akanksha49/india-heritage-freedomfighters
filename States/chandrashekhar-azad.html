Chandrasekhar Azad

Chandrasekhar Azad (1906-1931) was one of India's most fearless revolutionaries who vowed never to be captured alive by the British. A key figure in the armed resistance against colonial rule, he became a symbol of courage, sacrifice and revolutionary zeal.

Born

July 23, 1906

Bhabhra, Alirajpur State (now Madhya Pradesh)

Died

February 27, 1931 (aged 24)

Alfred Park, Allahabad (now Prayagraj), Uttar Pradesh

Original Name

Chandrasekhar Tiwari

Known As

"Azad" (The Free One), "Quick Silver"

"Dushman ki goliyon ka hum samna karenge, Azad hi rahein hain, Azad hi rahenge!"
("We will face the enemy's bullets, we are free and will remain free!")

Biography

Born as Chandrasekhar Tiwari in a small village in present-day Madhya Pradesh, Azad was deeply influenced by the Jallianwala Bagh massacre at age 13. He joined Mahatma Gandhi's Non-Cooperation Movement at 15, where he was arrested and famously declared his name as "Azad" (The Free One), his father's name as "Swatantrata" (Independence) and his residence as "Jail". This marked the beginning of his revolutionary journey.

After Gandhi called off the Non-Cooperation Movement following the Chauri Chaura incident, Azad became disillusioned with non-violence and turned to armed revolution. He joined the Hindustan Republican Association (later Hindustan Socialist Republican Association or HSRA) and became its chief strategist.

Azad was known for his brilliant guerilla tactics, ability to evade capture, and strict discipline. He trained young revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh, Sukhdev, and Rajguru. His most famous operation was the assassination of J.P. Saunders in 1928 to avenge the death of Lala Lajpat Rai.

On February 27, 1931, surrounded by British police in Allahabad's Alfred Park, Azad fought valiantly before shooting himself with his last bullet, staying true to his vow never to be captured alive. He was just 24 years old.

Key Revolutionary Activities

  • Kakori Train Robbery (1925): As part of HRA, participated in looting a train carrying British government money to fund revolutionary activities
  • Saunders' Assassination (1928): Planned and executed killing of British police officer J.P. Saunders to avenge Lala Lajpat Rai's death
  • Assembly Bombing (1929): Mentored Bhagat Singh and Batukeshwar Dutt in their bombing of Central Legislative Assembly
  • Building Network: Established secret revolutionary cells across North India and trained young recruits
  • Propaganda: Oversaw publication of revolutionary literature like "The Revolutionary"

Philosophy and Ideology

Chandrasekhar Azad's revolutionary philosophy was shaped by:

  • Belief in armed resistance against colonial oppression when non-violence failed
  • Commitment to socialist principles - wanted independent India to be socialist republic
  • Strict discipline and code of conduct for revolutionaries
  • Emphasis on self-sacrifice and complete dedication to the cause
  • Vision of India free from both British rule and social inequalities

He famously said: "If yet your blood does not rage, then it is water that flows in your veins. For what is the flush of youth, if it is not of service to the motherland?"

Lessons from Chandrasekhar Azad's Life

Azad's fearless resistance teaches us to stand firm against oppression regardless of odds.

Strategic Thinking

His careful planning shows the importance of strategy in achieving goals.

Mentorship

His guidance to younger revolutionaries like Bhagat Singh demonstrates leadership.

His ultimate sacrifice reminds us to stay true to our principles.

Teamwork

Adaptability

His shift from non-violence to armed resistance when needed shows pragmatic thinking.

Legacy and Memorials

Chandrasekhar Azad's legacy continues to inspire generations:

  • Alfred Park in Allahabad where he died was renamed Chandrasekhar Azad Park
  • The spot where he died in the park has a memorial with his statue
  • Many schools, colleges and roads across India are named after him
  • Several films and TV series have been made about his life
  • His birthplace Bhabhra was renamed Azadnagar in his honor
  • The Indian Coast Guard commissioned a ship named ICGS Chandrasekhar Azad

Azad remains one of India's most revered revolutionary figures, remembered for his courage, strategic mind, and ultimate sacrifice at a young age. His life continues to inspire patriotic fervor and commitment to justice.

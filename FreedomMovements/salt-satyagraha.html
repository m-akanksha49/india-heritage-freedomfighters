Salt Satyagraha

The Salt March

The Salt Satyagraha, also known as the Salt March or Dandi March, was a nonviolent protest led by Mahatma Gandhi against the British salt monopoly in colonial India. Beginning on March 12, 1930 and lasting until April 6, 1930, it became one of the most significant campaigns in the Indian independence movement.

Why Salt?

Gandhi chose salt as the focus of protest because it was a basic necessity used by all Indians, rich and poor alike. The British salt laws prohibited Indians from collecting or selling salt, forcing them to buy expensive, heavily taxed salt from British merchants. This simple act of defiance resonated with millions and became a powerful symbol of resistance.

Historical Timeline

Mar 12

March Begins

Gandhi sets out from Sabarmati Ashram with 78 carefully selected followers representing different regions of India. The march covered 240 miles to the coastal village of Dandi, with participants walking about 10 miles each day.

Apr 5

Arrival at Dandi

The marchers reach Dandi after 24 days of walking. Along the way, they stopped at villages where Gandhi gave speeches explaining the meaning of swaraj (self-rule) and the importance of challenging unjust laws.

Apr 6

Defying the Law

At dawn, Gandhi picks up a lump of natural salt from the beach, declaring "With this, I am shaking the foundations of the British Empire." This symbolic act sparked mass civil disobedience across India as millions began making or buying illegal salt.

May 4

Gandhi's Arrest

Gandhi is arrested after announcing his intention to raid the Dharasana Salt Works. His arrest led to worldwide condemnation of British rule in India and inspired even greater participation in the movement.

Deep Dive: The Salt Satyagraha Movement

The Salt Satyagraha was not just a protest against the salt tax, but a carefully planned campaign that combined political strategy with spiritual principles. Here's a closer look at various aspects of this historic movement:

Strategic Planning

Gandhi spent weeks planning the march, selecting participants from different regions and religions to represent India's diversity. The route was carefully chosen to maximize publicity and pass through areas where Congress support was strong.

Daily Routine

Marchers followed a strict daily schedule: waking at 4 AM for prayers, walking 10-12 miles daily, stopping at villages to spin khadi and give speeches. Gandhi used these stops to explain satyagraha and recruit volunteers.

International Coverage

Over 100 foreign journalists covered the march, including Webb Miller whose reports of police brutality at Dharasana shocked the world. Time magazine named Gandhi "Man of the Year" in 1930.

Economic Impact

The salt tax represented 8.2% of British tax revenue from India. The campaign caused salt revenue to drop by 75% in some areas as Indians began producing and selling illegal salt nationwide.

Women's Participation

Over 17,000 women were arrested during the campaign, marking their first mass participation in the freedom struggle. Leaders like Kamaladevi Chattopadhyay led salt-making demonstrations in Bombay.

Creative Protests

Beyond salt-making, protesters held "forest satyagrahas" collecting forbidden wood, manufactured illegal khadi cloth, and organized bonfires of foreign goods. Students left government schools and colleges.

Legacy of the Salt March

The Salt Satyagraha demonstrated the power of nonviolent resistance on a massive scale. It inspired similar campaigns worldwide, including the US Civil Rights Movement. Most importantly, it united Indians across caste, class and religious lines in a common struggle, making independence inevitable. The British Empire's moral authority was irreparably damaged by images of nonviolent protesters being beaten for making salt.

Key Figures

Mahatma Gandhi

Leader of the Salt Satyagraha and principal architect of India's nonviolent independence movement.

Sardar Patel

Key organizer who helped mobilize support across Gujarat during the Salt March.

Sarojini Naidu

Led the nonviolent raid on the Dharasana Salt Works after Gandhi's arrest.

Abbas Tyabji

Former chief justice who joined the movement and led the march after Gandhi's arrest.

Impact

  • Brought international attention to the Indian independence movement
  • Marked the first mass participation of women in civil disobedience
  • Demonstrated the power of nonviolent resistance against colonial rule
  • Forced the British government to negotiate with Gandhi as a representative of India
  • Inspired future civil rights movements worldwide, including the American Civil Rights Movement
  • Led to the Gandhi-Irwin Pact and participation in the Second Round Table Conference
